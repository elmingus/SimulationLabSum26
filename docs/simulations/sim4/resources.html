Acute Care AAC Toolkit

AAC includes any strategy, tool, or system used to supplement or replace speech.

In acute care, AAC is frequently used to support communication for patients experiencing:

  • Intubation or mechanical ventilation
  • Tracheostomy
  • Neurological injury
  • Motor impairment
  • Aphasia
  • Dysarthria
  • Hearing loss
  • Visual impairment
  • Temporary confusion
  • Fatigue
  • Language-access barriers

Low-Tech AAC Options

Low-tech AAC does not require electronic equipment.

Examples include:

  • Yes/no board
  • Alphabet board
  • Picture communication board
  • Pain scale
  • Body-part diagram
  • Written choice board
  • Communication notebook
  • Eye-gaze board
  • Partner-assisted scanning
  • Whiteboard
  • Gesture
  • Written key words

High-Tech AAC Options

High-tech AAC may include:

  • Tablet-based communication applications
  • Speech-generating devices
  • Eye-gaze speech-generating devices
  • Switch-access communication systems
  • Head-controlled devices
  • Text-to-speech applications
  • Personal voice amplifiers
Note

The most appropriate AAC method is the method the patient can use reliably, efficiently, and consistently. The most technologically advanced option is not always the best option.

Feature-Matching Considerations

Feature matching involves comparing the patient’s skills and needs with the characteristics of available AAC systems.

Patient Factor AAC Considerations
Motor ability Direct selection, eye gaze, switch access, partner-assisted scanning
Vision Target size, contrast, spacing, lighting, field of presentation
Hearing Written supports, amplification, visual cues
Language Vocabulary complexity, symbol type, literacy, preferred language
Cognition Number of choices, navigation demands, memory demands
Fatigue Physical effort, visual effort, need for backup methods
Medical status Positioning, respiratory support, alertness, pain
Communication needs Pain, comfort, choices, medical decisions, family contact
Environment Noise, staff familiarity, board placement, infection control

Establishing Yes/No Responses

A reliable yes/no response is often the foundation of functional communication.

Possible Response Modes

  • Verbal response
  • Head nod or shake
  • Eye movement
  • Blink response
  • Thumb movement
  • Finger movement
  • Pointing to written choices
  • Switch activation

Reliability Check

Ask several questions with known answers.

Include:

  • Questions for which the answer is yes
  • Questions for which the answer is no
  • Questions related to personal information
  • Questions unrelated to immediate wants or needs

Avoid relying on a single response.

Partner-Assisted Scanning

Partner-assisted scanning may be useful when the patient cannot directly point to a communication target.

The communication partner:

  1. Presents categories or choices.
  2. Pauses after each option.
  3. Watches for the patient’s established response.
  4. Confirms the selected message.
  5. Repeats the process as needed.

Example:

Are you uncomfortable because of pain, positioning, temperature, toileting, breathing, or something else?

Communication Partner Strategies

Staff and family should:

  • Gain the patient’s attention.
  • Face the patient.
  • Reduce distractions.
  • Use short, direct messages.
  • Ask one question at a time.
  • Allow adequate response time.
  • Verify yes/no responses.
  • Confirm the patient’s intended message.
  • Keep AAC materials within reach and view.
  • Document the established communication method.
  • Provide a backup communication strategy.

Interpreter Considerations

A qualified medical interpreter should be used for:

  • Medical assessment
  • Informed consent
  • Diagnosis and prognosis
  • Treatment education
  • Medical decision-making
  • Discharge planning
  • Complex symptom discussion

AAC may support immediate communication across languages but should not replace appropriate interpreter services.

Documentation Reminders

An acute care AAC note may describe:

Subjective

  • Reason for consultation
  • Patient or staff concerns
  • Patient-reported communication needs

Objective

  • Alertness
  • Command following
  • Yes/no reliability
  • Speech and language abilities
  • Reading and writing
  • Motor access
  • Vision and hearing
  • AAC methods trialed
  • Level of cueing or assistance
  • Patient accuracy and efficiency

Assessment

  • Primary communication barrier
  • Most effective access method
  • Functional implications
  • Need for skilled SLP services
  • Patient’s ability to communicate medical needs and decisions

Plan

  • Recommended AAC system
  • Staff and family education
  • Bedside signage
  • Interdisciplinary coordination
  • Follow-up
  • Need for additional assessment

Example Assessment Statement

Pt presents with impaired functional communication secondary to absent verbal output and severe motor limitations. Pt demonstrated reliable responses to concrete yes/no questions using upward eye gaze for yes and downward eye gaze for no. Pt successfully selected basic medical and comfort needs from a low-tech eye-gaze communication board with minimal cueing. Recommend continued use of established eye-gaze system with partner-assisted scanning available as a backup method.

Example Plan Statement

Continue skilled ST intervention to establish an efficient and reliable AAC system for communication of basic wants, medical needs, and care preferences. Provide ongoing staff and family education and coordinate with OT and nursing regarding positioning, access, and bedside implementation.

End-of-Simulation Reflection

After completing all four cases, consider:

  1. How did motor, sensory, cognitive, linguistic, and environmental factors affect AAC selection?

  2. When was low-tech AAC more appropriate than high-tech AAC?

  3. How did the immediate medical context influence your recommendations?

  4. What information would you communicate to nursing at the end of each consultation?

  5. What aspects of AAC assessment and implementation require additional practice?
