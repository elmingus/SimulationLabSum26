Case A: Quadriplegia and Loss of Speech

Benjamin Carter

38-year-old male | Consult: AAC Evaluation

SLP Task: Review the patient information below and determine the most appropriate AAC supports to facilitate functional communication in the acute care setting.

Patient Snapshot

Patient Information Details
Name Benjamin Carter
Age 38
Admitting Diagnosis C3–C4 spinal cord injury
Current Setting Intensive Care Unit
Respiratory Status Mechanically ventilated
Communication Status Unable to produce functional speech
Motor Status Quadriplegia with minimal voluntary head movement
Cognitive Status Alert and able to follow commands
Language English
Family Partner visits regularly

Chief Concern

The patient has frequently attempted to gain the attention of staff. Nursing reports that the patient appears frustrated and uncomfortable.

Through informal yes/no questioning, staff believe that the patient may be experiencing:

  • A headache
  • Discomfort related to positioning
  • A recent episode of urinary incontinence
  • A desire to speak with their partner

Nursing has not yet established a consistent communication system.

Immediate Clinical Concern: The patient currently lacks a reliable method for communicating pain, physical discomfort, hygiene needs, and requests for assistance.

Step 1: Identify the Communication Barrier

Consider the following:

  • Why is verbal communication unavailable?
  • What communication abilities appear to be preserved?
  • What information is still needed before selecting an AAC method?
  • Are the patient’s responses reliable and consistent?
  • Does the patient have sufficient vision for an eye-gaze system?
  • Can the patient move their eyes vertically and horizontally?
  • Is head movement reliable enough to use as an access method?
  • Can the patient tolerate sustained visual attention?

Step 2: Establish a Reliable Yes/No Response

Before introducing a larger communication system, determine whether the patient has a reliable yes/no response.

Possible response modes include:

  • Looking upward for yes and downward for no
  • Looking toward a written YES or NO
  • Blinking once for yes and twice for no
  • Moving the head in a consistent direction
  • Using partner-assisted scanning

Test the response using questions with known answers.

Examples:

  • Is your name Benjamin?
  • Are you currently in the hospital?
  • Are you 10 years old?
  • Is your partner’s name Maria?

A reliable yes/no response should be verified across multiple questions before it is used for medical decision-making.

Step 3: Evaluate Access Skills

Determine which access methods may be feasible.

Motor Access

  • Can the patient move either hand?
  • Can the patient point?
  • Can the patient move their head?
  • Can the patient activate a switch?
  • Are eye movements consistent?
  • Does fatigue affect performance?

Sensory Access

  • Can the patient see large written words?
  • Can the patient visually scan multiple options?
  • Does the patient require glasses?
  • Can the patient hear verbal choices?
  • Is background noise affecting performance?

Cognitive-Linguistic Skills

  • Can the patient follow one-step directions?
  • Can the patient read single words?
  • Can the patient identify pictures?
  • Can the patient make selections from a field of choices?
  • Can the patient maintain attention long enough to use a communication system?

Step 4: Select AAC Supports

Which supports would you trial?

  • Eye-gaze communication board
  • Large-print yes/no board
  • Partner-assisted auditory scanning
  • Pain-rating board
  • Body-part or positioning board
  • Alphabet board
  • Speech-generating device with eye-gaze access
  • Single-message switch
  • Call system adapted for head or eye movement

Consider:

  1. Which option offers the fastest access to urgent needs?
  2. Which option requires the least physical effort?
  3. Which option can nursing staff use consistently across shifts?
  4. What backup method should be available if the primary system fails?

Step 5: Address Immediate Needs

Using the patient’s established response method, determine the priority of the following concerns:

  • Headache
  • Repositioning
  • Incontinence and hygiene
  • Contacting the patient’s partner

Consider how you would communicate each need to the appropriate team member.

Patient Need Appropriate Follow-Up
Headache Notify nursing and support pain assessment
Positioning discomfort Notify nursing and/or physical or occupational therapy
Hygiene needs Notify nursing staff
Contact partner Confirm patient consent and assist with communication

Step 6: Interdisciplinary Coordination

Which team members may need to participate?

  • Nursing
  • Respiratory therapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Physical therapy
  • Physician or advanced practice provider
  • Family or communication partner
  • Assistive technology specialist

Consider what education should be placed at the bedside.

Examples:

  • The patient’s established yes/no response
  • Positioning instructions for the communication board
  • Instructions for partner-assisted scanning
  • A list of frequently requested messages
  • The patient’s preferred communication method
  • Instructions for verifying responses

Documentation Task

Complete a SOAP note that includes:

  • The reason for consultation
  • Communication and access skills assessed
  • Reliability of yes/no responses
  • AAC strategies trialed
  • Level of assistance required
  • Immediate needs identified
  • Patient and staff education
  • Interdisciplinary recommendations
  • Plan for follow-up

Reflection Questions

  1. How did you determine the order in which the patient’s immediate needs should be addressed?

  2. Which AAC method was most appropriate for this patient, and what patient-specific factors supported your choice?

  3. What backup communication method would you recommend if fatigue or visual changes reduced the patient’s eye-gaze accuracy?

Reveal Example Clinical Reasoning

The patient is alert, follows commands, and has severe motor limitations with no functional speech. Eye movement may represent the patient’s most reliable access method.

An initial low-tech system could include a large-print yes/no board and an eye-gaze communication board containing urgent messages such as pain, positioning, suction, bathroom, family, hot, cold, and call nurse.

Partner-assisted scanning could serve as a backup method. The communication partner would verbally present categories and choices while the patient uses the established yes/no response.

A high-tech eye-gaze speech-generating device could be considered if the communication impairment is expected to continue and the patient demonstrates adequate visual attention and language skills.

The most urgent concerns are pain, positioning, and hygiene. Nursing should be notified immediately, while the patient’s request to contact their partner should be addressed after consent is confirmed.
