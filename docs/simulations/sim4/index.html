Simulation 4: AAC Consultation

AAC Consultation

Acute Care Simulation | Consultation-Based Activity

Simulation Overview

Patients in acute care may experience temporary or permanent barriers to communication related to neurological injury, intubation, motor impairment, sensory loss, language differences, pain, fatigue, or changes in cognition.

In this simulation, you will assume the role of the consulting speech-language pathologist. You will review several patient scenarios, identify barriers to functional communication, and recommend appropriate augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) supports.

Unlike previous simulations, this activity is primarily completed through the course website rather than through a mannequin-based encounter.

Clinical Focus

The goal of an acute care AAC consultation is not necessarily to identify the most advanced communication device. The immediate goal is to establish a reliable and efficient way for the patient to communicate medical needs, preferences, questions, and decisions.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this simulation, students will be able to:

  • Identify barriers affecting functional communication in the acute care setting.
  • Evaluate patient access abilities, including motor, visual, auditory, cognitive, and linguistic factors.
  • Select appropriate low-tech and high-tech AAC supports.
  • Develop strategies for communicating urgent medical and personal needs.
  • Provide patient, family, and staff education regarding AAC use.
  • Coordinate recommendations with the interdisciplinary team.
  • Document findings and recommendations using an acute care SOAP note.
  • Reflect on clinical reasoning and patient-centered decision-making.

Your Role

You are the speech-language pathologist responding to a communication consultation.

For each patient case, you will:

  1. Review the reason for consultation.
  2. Identify the primary communication barrier.
  3. Evaluate available communication and access skills.
  4. Select AAC strategies to trial.
  5. Address the patient’s immediate communication needs.
  6. Identify appropriate interdisciplinary follow-up.
  7. Document your assessment and recommendations.

AAC Assessment Framework

When selecting an AAC approach, consider the following areas:

Area Questions to Consider
Communication Can the patient speak, vocalize, gesture, write, read, or answer yes/no questions?
Language Does the patient understand spoken and written language? What is the patient’s preferred language?
Cognition Can the patient attend, remember directions, make selections, and understand cause and effect?
Motor Access Can the patient point, reach, lift a hand, move their head, or use eye gaze?
Vision Can the patient see written words, pictures, colors, and communication targets?
Hearing Can the patient hear conversational speech? Are hearing aids or amplification available?
Environment Are noise, lighting, positioning, fatigue, or medical equipment affecting communication?
Urgency What medical, safety, comfort, or personal needs must be communicated immediately?
